Overview

The Central Question

We study the ubiquitin-proteasome system, a cellular machinery of over 700 enzymes that controls the stability, activity, and localization of thousands of proteins. When this system is disrupted, the consequences include cancer, cardiovascular disease, and neurodegeneration. Our work combines mechanistic biochemistry, disease-focused cell biology, and genome engineering to understand these disruptions and exploit them therapeutically.

Focus Areas

Research Programs

Four interconnected programs spanning fundamental mechanisms to therapeutic applications.

Ubiquitination in Cancer: Mechanisms and Vulnerabilities

E3 ubiquitin ligases and deubiquitinases (DUBs) are critical regulators of proteins that cancer cells depend on for survival and proliferation. The laboratory dissects these regulatory relationships to identify cancer-specific vulnerabilities within the ubiquitin network. Current projects investigate the USP46 deubiquitinase in HPV-driven cervical cancer, the CRL4-Cdt2 ubiquitin ligase complex in breast cancer proliferation, and the role of extrachromosomal DNA amplifications in oncogenesis and drug resistance in cervical cancer.

Target Identification and Drug Discovery in the Ubiquitin Network

The laboratory identifies targets that are essential for cancer survival and druggable within the ubiquitination network, and employs structure-guided drug design and in vitro screening pipelines to develop mechanism-based therapeutics. Current drug discovery efforts are focused on small-molecule inhibitors of USP46, integrating active site structural analysis and compound library screening to identify and optimize lead candidates.

Deubiquitinases in Cardiovascular Physiology

Protein stability within the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system is poorly understood at the post-translational level. The laboratory investigates how deubiquitinases regulate the steady-state levels of the angiotensin II type 1 receptor (AT1R) and the alpha-1D adrenergic receptor, two key determinants of vascular tone and blood pressure. This work, supported by an ICMR research grant, aims to define ubiquitin-dependent mechanisms governing receptor homeostasis and their relevance to cardiovascular pathophysiology.

CRISPR-Based Genome Editing: From Mechanism to Disease Models

A central question in studying endogenous protein function is how to precisely engineer both copies of a gene in diploid cells. The laboratory is developing strategies for efficient biallelic knock-in editing using CRISPR-Cas9, with ubiquitination pathway genes serving as the primary model system. The resulting platform is applied to build accurate cellular and animal models of genetic disease, and to evaluate CRISPR-based correction of pathogenic mutations as a therapeutic strategy.
